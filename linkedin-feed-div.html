    "There is now more vibe coders than users of their products, ha ha".

    Just one of many toxic, unregulated nervous system responses to a new technology. But let's stress-test it for a sec.

    Most software, contrary to popular belief, falls under the category of low adoption. Built for one user, a couple or a couple dozen.

    - Niche open-source packages
    - Scripts, macros
    - Internal tools
    - School or study projects

    For most of software history, people wrote code to learn, to play, to automate one task, to run one experiment. We think it's not because of a handful of global monopolies – but there's more software than that.

    In 1999, I built a "design program" in QBASIC, called "Super Draw 2000". (Get it?) It allowed you to draw rectangles, circles and lines, and save them to a file. I felt great. No one ever saw it.

    Then I half-built a couple of games with a few people from a Visual Basic forum. My first game that felt like a game was a submarine shooter – I enjoyed building it, and my audience of 2 or 3 friends said they liked it too. Never felt bad for not having 100M downloads/week. I learned a lot, played a lot.

    What vibe coding critics miss by a mile is that building software can and should be fun, and personal. Just because venture capital distorted our idea of value and growth to mean global dominance and winner takes all mentality, it doesn't mean that building for yourself or your friends isn't valuable, or valid. It is, and always has been.

    Building AI agents is *a lot more* UX design than engineering. I would even argue it is pure distilled UX design, but I want to be careful with hyperbolas.

    Let me explain.

    On the surface, agents = no screens = why the heck you need designers anymore? Sure, voice is a thing but that's about it?

    (Btw, I'm learning this directly by building my own, collaboration-first agentic AI framework. More on that some other time.)

    Here are some decisions you are making when building agents for complex work:

    - What is an agent in your case? What problem does it solve? How do you define those problems for n customers without ending up with a generic system prompt that misses the nuance?

    - How and when do you split a problem into multiple agents? What roles do they have that makes them necessary? I.e. why not just a single agent?

    - What capabilities do you want agents to do? This isn't just a technical question (they can have any tool). This is a behavior design question. It makes or breaks trust, not just raw capability.

    - What is the agent loop? How the agent goes around to solve the problem is a part of the behavior design. Ralph Wiggum loop is an example of how this space is not "solved".

    - How does an agent communicate with one another? What is the power dynamic? Handoff? What if the sub-agent challenges the calling agent? The agents already have a dynamic built in – the underlying LLM bias – so you can't get away from building or inheriting an equivalent of a social system. Weird, I know.

    - How do you prevent bias, introduce a critical voice, push back onto the user? "You're absolutely right!" is the equivalent of Microsoft's "Remember my choice" that never remembers your choice.

    - What kind of language does each agent use? Utilitarian, why? Is it chatty or does it just run 20 tools and says Done? Why? Which customer segments benefit from which approach?

    - What role does memory play for your agents? How do you decide what parts of the conversation get stored in memory and when? How do you prevent past poor decisions from rotting future novel problems?

    - When dealing with expanding context, which strategy is the most appropriate and why? "Compress" everything? Brute-force cut-off? Or preserve specific nuance important for the task? How does your audience define "memory-worthy"? If users get annoyed at you remembering weird details, that's a UX problem. (Looking at you, ChatGPT Memory)

    - What persona do your agents take? Not in the salesy sense but how do they visibly communicate and influence outcomes using specific skills? Or is their persona just a facade while the outcomes are bland like any other hashtag#agent?

    There's a gazillion more. Not even mentioning the UI of interacting with agents.

    You might argue those are largely engineering concerns. They're not – they shape customer experience, they influence direct outcomes, they differentiate from competition.

    I never had a stable morning routine at work. A perfect checklist, a sequence of tabs. Will this AI morning brief become one?

    Claude Code helped me put together a workflow where:

    - Every morning at 7am
    - Gets current and next iteration issues from Github (content + comments)
    - Stores as Markdown files in a folder structure that replicates boards/statuses
    - Then writes up a report
    - And sends to me on Slack.

    The report looks at what's new and prioritizes a checklist:

    1. Any release-related work that is blocking, hanging, waiting on me
    2. Any comments that @ me but I haven't responded yet
    3. Any threads where I might need to jump in, where conversation is hanging etc.

    Next up: Hooking up Google Calendar to surface any recent transcripts and their action items that I still need to take care of.

    It took me 30 minutes to get it to some shape, and about 2 more hours of fiddling with details. If I knew what I wanted right away, Claude could've one-shotted it in 10 minutes easily.

    If you think you're 'falling behind' on these things, you're maybe several hours away from learning 80% of what you need to learn. Don't let LinkedIn algo convince you otherwise.

    There's a lot of noise from the process and operations-oriented people in the PM community about using Claude Code for Product work.

    Claude Code is at the cutting edge of agentic AI currently so it's a great way to immerse yourself into what's possible. It's not the only tool, but the ecosystem around it is at the highest pace of innovation right now, and that matters.

    However, this gets easily twisted by the LinkedIn algorithm favoring rage baits and hyperbolas, and influencer content becomes widespread News Feed pollution where signal can get lost.

    To me, the signal is:

    - You need to be at the cutting edge because your job is to be at the cutting edge in order to feed your product sense with more data points. Product sense is like a diet. You feed your Product Gut Biome with variety so your instincts are sharper.

    - There are different cutting edges though. Process people will say the cutting edge is using MCPs with local files to organize tickets or draft PRDs. Great. Technology cutting edge would be around distilling SLMs or building agentic frameworks from scratch. Business cutting edge might be building AI pricing models and scenarios with AI. What gets hyped as "be here or die as a PM" is a cutting edge in someone's localized sphere of interest + LinkedIn algorithm's twisted effects.

    - The danger isn't to not learn a tool like Claude, it is to not understand the dynamics that make it attractive, effective or ineffective. Everyone eventually learned Word but few people write well. Everyone now uses AI to write, yet you can tell half-baked ideas from deeply developed ones.

    - And finally, there's probably no better time for all of us to learn to regulate our emotions. You won't see that in your feed but taking care of yourself is important. Unregulated nervous systems won't do well with changes that are happening.
